# Chinese characters — hanzi and radicals

Chinese writing is one of the oldest in the world, still used today by over one billion people. Unlike an alphabet, each **hanzi** (Chinese character) represents a syllable and carries its own meaning.

:::definition
A **hanzi** (汉字 hànzì, literally 'Han character') is the basic unit of Chinese writing. Each hanzi corresponds to one syllable and has its own meaning. Dictionaries contain over 50,000, but **3,000 characters** are enough to read a newspaper, and approximately **150 to 200** are expected at BEPC level.
:::

## Brief history

:::propriete
| Period | Script | Description |
|---|---|---|
| ~1200 BCE | 甲骨文 jiǎgǔwén | Oracle bone script, pictograms carved on bones |
| Han era (~200 BCE) | 隶书 lìshū | Clerical style, more square-shaped |
| Modern era | 楷书 kǎishū | Regular style — basis of today's writing |
| 1956 (PRC) | 简体字 jiǎntǐzì | Simplified characters (fewer strokes) |
| Taiwan/HK | 繁體字 fántǐzì | Traditional characters (more strokes) |

In Cameroon, **simplified characters** are taught (official system of mainland China).
:::

## The 8 basic strokes

:::propriete
Every character is made up of **strokes** (笔画 bǐhuà). There are 8 fundamental strokes:

| Stroke | Name | Pinyin | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| — | Horizontal | héng 横 | 一, 二, 三 |
| \| | Vertical | shù 竖 | 中, 十 |
| ノ | Left-falling | piě 撇 | 人, 八 |
| ＼ | Right-falling | nà 捺 | 人, 木 |
| · | Dot | diǎn 点 | 六, 心 |
| ∟ | Bent/Hook | zhé-gōu 折钩 | 口, 子 |
| ↗ | Rising | tí 提 | 地, 打 |
:::

## Stroke order — fundamental rules

:::methode
To write a character correctly, follow these rules in order:

1. **Top to bottom**: write strokes at the top first, then those at the bottom.
2. **Left to right**: write strokes on the left before those on the right.
3. **Horizontal before vertical**: at an intersection, the horizontal stroke comes first.
4. **Outside before inside**: write the enclosing strokes before the enclosed content.
5. **Close last**: the closing stroke of a frame is drawn last.

Example: 国 (guó, country) — first the outer frame □, then the content 玉, then close the bottom.
:::

## Radicals — the key to meaning

:::definition
A **radical** (部首 bùshǒu) is a basic graphic component that often indicates the **semantic category** of a character. There are **214 official radicals**. Recognising radicals helps you guess the meaning of an unknown character.
:::

:::propriete
| Radical | Pinyin | Meaning | Example characters |
|---|---|---|---|
| 氵 | shuǐ (water) | related to water | 海 hǎi (sea), 河 hé (river), 湖 hú (lake) |
| 木 | mù (wood) | related to trees | 树 shù (tree), 桌 zhuō (table), 椅 yǐ (chair) |
| 口 | kǒu (mouth) | related to speech | 叫 jiào (to call), 吃 chī (to eat), 说 shuō (to speak) |
| 人/亻 | rén (person) | related to people | 他 tā (he), 你 nǐ (you), 们 men (plural suffix) |
| 心/忄 | xīn (heart) | related to emotions | 想 xiǎng (to think), 爱 ài (to love), 怕 pà (to fear) |
| 火/灬 | huǒ (fire) | related to fire/heat | 热 rè (hot), 烧 shāo (to burn), 煮 zhǔ (to cook) |
| 女 | nǚ (woman) | related to women/family | 妈 mā (mum), 姐 jiě (older sister), 她 tā (she) |
:::

:::exemple
**The logic of compound characters:**

- 木 (mù, wood) + 木 = 林 (lín, small wood/forest)
- 木 + 木 + 木 = 森 (sēn, dense forest)
- 日 (rì, sun) + 月 (yuè, moon) = 明 (míng, bright/clear)
- 女 (nǚ, woman) + 子 (zǐ, child) = 好 (hǎo, good)

This logic lets you guess the meaning of many characters!
:::

:::attention
**Simplified** (mainland China) and **traditional** (Taiwan/Hong Kong) characters are different. Examples:
- 爱 (simplified) = 愛 (traditional) = love
- 国 (simplified) = 國 (traditional) = country

In the Cameroonian BEPC, **simplified characters** are used.
:::

:::retenir
- One hanzi = 1 syllable + 1 meaning. ~3,000 are sufficient for everyday reading.
- **8 basic strokes**: horizontal, vertical, left/right-falling, dot, bent, rising.
- Stroke order: top→bottom, left→right, horizontal before vertical.
- **214 radicals**: they indicate the semantic category of the character.
- Key examples: 氵(water), 木 (wood), 口 (mouth), 心 (heart), 人 (person).
:::
