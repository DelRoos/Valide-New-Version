# Responsible use of digital technology

Being connected is not enough — one must be connected responsibly. Digital technology opens up rights (information, expression, creation) but also imposes duties (respect for others, respect for the law, verification of information). **Digital citizenship** means knowing how to act online as one would act in society.

:::definition
**Digital citizenship** is the set of responsible, respectful and ethical behaviours in the use of digital technologies. It covers: respect for others online, verification of information, respect for intellectual property, and the balance between digital life and real life.
:::

## Cyberbullying

:::definition
**Cyberbullying** is a form of harassment that uses digital tools (social networks, SMS, email, online games) to persecute a person repeatedly and intentionally. It can take several forms:

- **Repeated offensive messages**: insults, threats by SMS or messaging.
- **Public humiliation**: sharing embarrassing photos or mockery.
- **Exclusion**: blocking someone from a group, ignoring them in a coordinated way.
- **Identity theft**: creating a fake profile to harm someone.
- **Cyberstalking**: monitoring and harassing a person's online activities.
:::

:::propriete
Consequences of cyberbullying:

- **For the victim**: anxiety, depression, isolation, refusal to go to school, in severe cases: suicidal thoughts.
- **For the perpetrator**: school disciplinary sanctions, legal proceedings (law n°2010/012 in Cameroon).
- Online harassment is punishable by law, even when it occurs between minors.
:::

:::methode
Facing cyberbullying — whether you are a victim or a witness:

1. **Do not respond** to aggressors (ignoring them removes their power; responding encourages them).
2. **Save evidence**: screenshots, dates, times, names of senders.
3. **Block** harassing individuals on the platforms concerned.
4. **Report** the content to the platform (the «Report» option on all social networks).
5. **Talk to a trusted adult**: parent, teacher, school counsellor.
6. **Contact the authorities** if the situation is serious: police or ANTIC in Cameroon.
:::

## Copyright and intellectual property

:::definition
**Copyright** (or author's rights) automatically protects any original work from its creation, without any administrative process:

- **Protected works**: texts, music, films, photographs, software, drawings, video games, databases.
- **Protection**: the author is the only one who can reproduce, distribute, modify or sell their work.
- **Duration**: as a general rule, 70 years after the author's death (varies by country).

Using, reproducing or sharing a protected work without authorisation is illegal and can lead to sanctions.
:::

:::propriete
| Action | Legality |
|---|---|
| Downloading music on a legal platform (Spotify, Apple Music) | ✅ Legal |
| Sharing a PDF of a school textbook in a WhatsApp group | ❌ Illegal (copyright) |
| Copy-pasting a text from Wikipedia without citing it | ❌ Plagiarism |
| Using an image found on Google in a school project | ❌ Illegal without authorisation |
| Listening to free music on YouTube | ✅ Legal (adverts = remuneration) |
| Downloading a film with uTorrent | ❌ Illegal (piracy) |
:::

## Free licences (Creative Commons)

:::definition
**Creative Commons** (CC) licences are legal tools allowing authors to share their works while precisely defining the conditions of use. Unlike strict copyright, they allow certain uses without individual authorisation.

Examples of CC licences:
- **CC BY**: free use if the author is cited.
- **CC BY-SA**: free use with citation + sharing under the same conditions.
- **CC BY-NC**: free use with citation but **not** for commercial use.
- **CC0**: public domain — no restriction on use.

Works under a CC licence are legally usable if one respects the specified conditions.
:::

## Disinformation and verification of information

:::methode
On the Internet, false information (fake news) and true news mix together. To verify information:

1. **Check the source**: who wrote this article? Is the site recognised (official institution, reputable media)?
2. **Look for other sources**: do other serious media report the same information?
3. **Check the date**: is the information recent or taken out of context?
4. **Verify images** with Google Images (reverse search): is the photo authentic or reused?
5. **Use fact-checking websites** (fact verification): AFP Factuel, Snopes, etc.
6. **Beware of shocking headlines**: false information often plays on emotions.
:::

## Digital balance

:::propriete
Responsible use of digital technology also includes finding a **balance** between the online world and the real world.

| Sign of problematic use | Advice |
|---|---|
| Difficulty stopping scrolling | Set screen-free time slots |
| Sleep disturbed by screens | Turn off screens 1 hour before sleeping |
| Anxiety without access to the phone | Practise offline activities (sport, reading) |
| Neglecting real friends and family | Prioritise moments of physical presence |
:::

:::retenir
- **Digital citizenship** = acting online with the same values as in real society.
- **Cyberbullying**: harassment via digital tools, repeated and intentional — illegal and punishable.
- Facing harassment: **do not respond**, **save evidence**, **report**, **talk to an adult**.
- **Copyright**: automatically protects any original work against unauthorised copying.
- **Creative Commons**: licences allowing legal sharing under conditions defined by the author.
- Before sharing information online: **verify the source** so as not to spread fake news.
:::
