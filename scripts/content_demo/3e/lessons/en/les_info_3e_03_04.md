# Privacy and personal data

Every day, often without realising it, we leave digital traces: a like, a search, a published photo. These traces constitute our **personal data** and can be collected, analysed, or even used against us. Knowing how to protect them is an essential skill.

:::definition
**Personal data** is all information that directly or indirectly identifies a living natural person. It includes:

- **Direct data**: surname, first name, national identity number, phone number, email address, photo.
- **Indirect data**: IP address, browsing cookies, GPS location, banking data.
- **Sensitive data** (enhanced protection): ethnic origin, political opinions, religion, health, biometric data.
:::

## The digital footprint

:::definition
A **digital footprint** is the set of traces a person leaves during their activities on the Internet:

- **Active traces**: posts on social networks, comments, service registrations, emails sent, shared photos.
- **Passive traces**: browsing history, cookies, IP address recorded by websites, geolocation.

This data is often collected by companies for **targeted advertising**, sold to third parties, or stolen by hackers.
:::

:::exemple
When Amara uses her smartphone, she leaves traces:
- She searches for «shoes» on Google → shoe advertisements appear on Facebook.
- She registers on a social network with her photo → facial recognition becomes possible.
- Her phone records her GPS location → her mobile operator knows her daily journeys.
- She watches YouTube videos → her interest profile is analysed by Google.

All of this often without her being aware of it.
:::

## Rights over personal data

:::propriete
Under data protection laws, every person has fundamental rights over their data:

| Right | Description |
|---|---|
| **Right of access** | Obtain a copy of all data held about oneself |
| **Right of rectification** | Have inaccurate or incomplete data corrected |
| **Right to erasure** | Request deletion of one's data («right to be forgotten») |
| **Right to object** | Object to the use of one's data for marketing purposes |
| **Right to portability** | Retrieve one's data in a reusable format |
:::

## Social networks and young people

:::propriete
Social networks present specific risks for young people:

| Risk | Description |
|---|---|
| **Overexposure** | Publishing too much personal information visible to all |
| **Cyberbullying** | Hurtful comments, mockery, exclusion from groups |
| **Grooming** | Manipulation by a malicious adult online |
| **Identity theft** | Creating a fake profile with one's photo or name |
| **Right to one's image** | Sharing a photo of someone without their permission |
:::

:::attention
**A photo published on the Internet can remain there forever.** Even after deletion, it may have been copied, shared or indexed by search engines. Before publishing a photo or text, ask yourself: «Would I be comfortable if my parents, teachers or future employer saw this?»
:::

## Good data protection practices

:::methode
To protect your personal data:

1. **Check the privacy settings** of each application and social network.
2. **Limit the information shared** online: do not publish your address, phone number or schedule.
3. **Read privacy policies** (even if long) before registering for a service.
4. **Refuse non-essential cookies** on websites.
5. **Use pseudonyms** on public platforms rather than your real name.
6. **Report** any content that uses your data without authorisation.
7. **Think before you post**: what is posted online can remain there indefinitely.
:::

## Cybersecurity for young people in Cameroon

:::exemple
In Cameroon, law n°2010/012 on cybersecurity and cybercrime protects citizens in the digital space. It governs in particular:

- The collection and processing of personal data (articles 9 onwards).
- Online offences: identity theft, distribution of illegal content.
- Protection of minors against harmful content.

In the event of a digital incident (cyberbullying, identity theft, hacking), it is possible to contact the cyber police or the National Agency for Information and Communication Technologies (ANTIC).
:::

:::retenir
- **Personal data** identifies a person: name, photo, email, location, etc.
- The **digital footprint** is the set of traces (active and passive) left on the Internet.
- Fundamental rights: **access**, **rectification**, **erasure**, **objection**, **portability**.
- Social network risks: cyberbullying, overexposure, identity theft, right to one's image.
- Golden rule: **before posting, think about the consequences — a digital trace can last indefinitely**.
:::
