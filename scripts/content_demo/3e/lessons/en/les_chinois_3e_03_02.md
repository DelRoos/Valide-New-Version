# Chinese gastronomy

Chinese cuisine is one of the richest and most diverse in the world. With over 3,000 years of culinary history, it reflects the geography, climate and culture of each region. In China, eating is a fundamental social and cultural act: 你吃了吗？Nǐ chī le ma? (Have you eaten?) is a common greeting!

## The eight great regional cuisines (八大菜系)

:::definition
China has **八大菜系 bā dà càixì** (eight great regional cuisines), each with its characteristic flavours, techniques and ingredients. This diversity is comparable to that of all of culinary Europe combined.

| Cuisine | Character | Pinyin | Province | Main characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cantonese | 粤菜 | Yuècài | Guangdong | Mild flavours, dim sum, steaming, freshness |
| Sichuanese | 川菜 | Chuāncài | Sichuan | Very spicy, Sichuan pepper (numbing), chilli |
| Pekingese | 京菜 | Jīngcài | Beijing | Peking duck, salty flavours, imperial influences |
| Shanghainese | 沪菜 | Hùcài | Shanghai | Sweet-salty, rich sauces, seafood |
| Hunanese | 湘菜 | Xiāngcài | Hunan | Spicy and sour, smoked, lots of red chilli |
| Fujianese | 闽菜 | Mǐncài | Fujian | Seafood, umami flavours, clear soups |
| Anhui | 徽菜 | Huīcài | Anhui | Wild herbs, braises, deep flavours |
| Shandong | 鲁菜 | Lǔcài | Shandong | Imperial court cuisine, seafood, broths |
:::

## North and South: two food cultures

:::propriete
China is divided by a **major dietary difference** between North and South:

| Aspect | North (北方 Běifāng) | South (南方 Nánfāng) |
|---|---|---|
| Staple food | 面食 miànshí (wheat, noodles, breads) | 米饭 mǐfàn (rice) |
| Flavours | Salty, hearty | Mild, sweet-umami |
| Typical dishes | Dumplings 饺子, pancakes 煎饼 | Fried rice 炒饭, rice porridge 粥 |
| Reason | Wheat grows better in the North (cold and dry) | Rice grows better in the South (warm and humid) |

**Chinese saying**: 南米北面 Nán mǐ Běi miàn = "The South eats rice, the North eats noodles"
:::

## Chopstick culture (筷子 kuàizi)

:::methode
**筷子 kuàizi** (chopsticks) have been used in China for over 3,000 years. Learning to use them is a sign of cultural respect.

**Rules of use:**

1. **Hold correctly**: lower chopstick fixed (rests on hand), upper chopstick mobile (pinches).
2. **Never stand chopsticks vertically in rice** — it resembles incense sticks at a funeral → very bad omen ❌
3. **Do not point chopsticks at someone** — considered rude.
4. **Rest on chopstick holder** (筷子架 kuàizi jià) between bites — never balanced on the bowl's edge.
5. **Do not spear food** with chopsticks like a fork — use a spoon 勺子 sháozi for soups.

**Comparison with Cameroon:** Cameroonians often use the right hand to eat traditional dishes. In China, chopsticks play that same cultural role of "touching food directly".
:::

## Tea culture (茶 chá)

:::definition
**Tea** (茶 chá) is China's national drink, with over 4,000 years of history. China is the world's leading producer and exporter of tea.

**The main tea families:**

| Type | Character | Pinyin | Fermentation | Flavour | Region |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Green tea | 绿茶 | lǜ chá | Unfermented | Fresh, vegetal | Zhejiang, Jiangsu |
| Black tea | 红茶 | hóng chá | Fermented | Robust, sweet | Yunnan, Fujian |
| Oolong tea | 乌龙茶 | wūlóng chá | Semi-fermented | Floral, fruity | Fujian, Taiwan |
| Pu'er tea | 普洱茶 | pǔ'ěr chá | Post-fermented | Earthy, deep | Yunnan |
| White tea | 白茶 | bái chá | Lightly oxidised | Delicate, sweet | Fujian |

**Note**: 红茶 hóng chá = literally "red tea" (not "black tea"). Chinese name tea by the colour of the brewed liquid, not the leaves.

**Tea ceremony** (茶道 chádào): traditional art of preparing and serving tea — a symbol of respect and hospitality.
:::

:::exemple
**Dialogue at the restaurant:**

Amara: 服务员，我们想点菜。
        Fúwùyuán, wǒmen xiǎng diǎn cài.
        Waiter, we would like to order.

Waiter: 好的，你们想吃什么？
         Hǎo de, nǐmen xiǎng chī shénme?
         Of course, what would you like to eat?

Amara: 有没有不辣的菜？我不能吃辣。
        Yǒu méiyǒu bù là de cài? Wǒ bù néng chī là.
        Do you have any non-spicy dishes? I can't eat spicy food.

Waiter: 有！我们有北京烤鸭和清蒸鱼。
         Yǒu! Wǒmen yǒu Běijīng kǎoyā hé qīngzhēng yú.
         Yes! We have Peking duck and steamed fish.

Amara: 好，我要北京烤鸭。还有一壶绿茶，谢谢！
        Hǎo, wǒ yào Běijīng kǎoyā. Hái yǒu yī hú lǜ chá, xièxie!
        Great, I'll have the Peking duck. And also a pot of green tea, thank you!
:::

:::attention
**Cultural differences to be aware of:**
- In China, **burping after a meal** is not considered rude (a sign of appreciation in some regions).
- **Finishing your plate entirely** can sometimes signal you didn't have enough → leaving a little is sometimes more polite in formal settings.
- People **do not drink during the meal** (tradition); soup broth replaces a drink.
- **Soup is often served at the end of the meal** in China (unlike in Europe where it comes first).
:::

:::retenir
- 八大菜系 bā dà càixì: eight great regional cuisines.
- North = wheat/noodles (面食 miànshí); South = rice (米饭 mǐfàn).
- Key dishes: 北京烤鸭 (Peking duck), 火锅 (hotpot), 饺子 (dumplings), 麻婆豆腐 (mapo tofu).
- 筷子 kuàizi (chopsticks): never stand vertically in rice (funeral omen).
- Tea: 绿茶 (green), 红茶 (black/red), 普洱茶 (pu'er), 乌龙茶 (oolong).
- Restaurant phrases: 服务员！(waiter!) / 我想点菜 (I'd like to order) / 买单！(the bill!).
:::
